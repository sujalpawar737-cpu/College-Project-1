Clear, step-by-step guidance for common emergencies

Use this as a quick helper, but always seek professional medical care.

üíì CPR - Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è Call 102 immediately if the person is unresponsive or not breathing. Start CPR while help is on the way. Continue until professional help arrives or the person starts breathing normally.
  • Check responsiveness: Tap the person's shoulders and shout, "Are you okay?".
  • Call for help: Dial 102 or ask someone nearby to call while you begin CPR.
  • Lay them on a flat surface: Place the person on their back on a firm, flat surface.
  • Open the airway: Tilt the head slightly back and lift the chin.
  • Hand position: Place the heel of one hand in the center of the chest, then place the other hand on top and interlock fingers.
  • Start chest compressions: Push hard and fast, about 5 cm deep, at 100‚Äì120 compressions per minute.
  • Give rescue breaths (if trained): After 30 compressions, give 2 breaths by sealing their mouth and blowing in for 1 second each.
  • Continue cycles: Repeat 30 compressions and 2 breaths without long pauses.
  • Use AED if available: Follow device voice prompts until medical help takes over.

ü´Å Choking - Airway Obstruction

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è If the person cannot speak, cough, or breathe, act immediately and call 102. Encourage coughing if they can still breathe and make sounds.
  • Confirm choking: Ask "Are you choking?". If they nod or cannot talk, proceed quickly.
  • Stand behind the person: Wrap your arms around their waist.
  • Make a fist: Place your fist just above the navel (belly button), thumb side in.
  • Grasp your fist: With your other hand, hold your fist firmly.
  • Perform upward thrusts: Pull sharply inward and upward into the abdomen.
  • Repeat thrusts: Continue until the object is expelled or the person becomes unconscious.
  • If unconscious: Gently lower them to the floor and begin CPR. Check the mouth for an object before giving breaths.
  • After clearing: Even if the person recovers, recommend a medical check-up.

ü©∏ Severe Bleeding - Heavy Cuts & Wounds

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è Heavy bleeding can be life-threatening. Call 102 right away. Act quickly to slow down or stop the bleeding while waiting for help.
  • Protect yourself: If possible, wear gloves or use a clean cloth to avoid contact with blood.
  • Apply direct pressure: Press firmly on the wound using a clean cloth or bandage.
  • Maintain pressure: Do not remove the cloth if it soaks through; place another one on top.
  • Elevate the limb: Raise the injured area above heart level if there is no fracture.
  • Use a bandage: Wrap the wound firmly, but not so tight that it cuts off circulation.
  • Tourniquet (only if needed): For life-threatening limb bleeding, tie a cloth a few centimeters above the wound and tighten.
  • Note the time: Remember when the tourniquet was applied to inform medical staff.
  • Monitor the person: Keep them lying down, calm, and warm until help arrives.

üî• Burns - Heat Injury

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è For large, deep, or facial burns, call 102 immediately. Never apply ice, toothpaste, oil, or ghee on burns.
  • Stop the burning: Move the person away from the heat source, fire, or hot object.
  • Cool the burn: Hold the burned area under cool running water for 15‚Äì20 minutes.
  • Remove tight items: Carefully remove jewelry or tight clothing before swelling starts.
  • Do not break blisters: Blisters protect the skin and should be left intact.
  • Cover the burn: Use a clean, non-stick cloth or sterile gauze.
  • Avoid home remedies: Do not apply butter, creams, or powders on the burn.
  • Pain relief: If the person is conscious, they may take a pain reliever like paracetamol (if not allergic).
  • Seek medical help: For burns on the face, hands, groin, or large areas, get professional care.

ü¶¥ Fractures - Broken Bones

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è Suspected fractures, especially with deformity or severe pain, require medical attention. Call 102 if serious. Avoid moving the injured area unnecessarily.
  • Check for signs: Pain, swelling, bruising, or abnormal shape of a limb.
  • Keep the person still: Ask them not to move the injured part.
  • Immobilize the limb: Use a splint (like a board, rolled magazine, or stick) along the injured area.
  • Secure the splint: Tie it gently using cloth strips or bandages above and below the injury.
  • Do not straighten the limb: If it's bent or deformed, do not try to realign it.
  • Apply ice (wrapped): Place a cloth-wrapped ice pack near the injury to reduce swelling.
  • Elevate if possible: Raise the injured area slightly, if it does not cause pain.
  • Seek hospital care: Get X-rays and professional treatment as soon as possible.

üêç Snake Bite - Venomous Bite

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è Treat all snake bites as serious. Call 102 immediately. Try to keep the person calm and still while arranging quick transport to a hospital.
  • Move to safety: Take the person away from the snake to avoid a second bite.
  • Keep them calm: Reassure the person and ask them not to move the bitten limb.
  • Remove tight items: Take off rings, watches, or tight clothes near the bite area.
  • Immobilize the limb: Keep the bite area still and at or slightly below heart level.
  • Apply a firm bandage: Wrap a broad bandage over the bite and along the limb (not too tight).
  • Use a splint: Support the limb with a splint to reduce movement.
  • Do NOT: Do not cut the wound, suck the venom, or apply ice or chemicals.
  • Note details: If safe, remember the snake's color and shape to inform doctors.
  • Reach hospital: Antivenom is given only at hospitals under doctor supervision.

‚ù§Ô∏è Heart Attack - Chest Pain / Discomfort

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è Chest pain that lasts more than a few minutes can be a heart attack. Call 102 immediately. Do not ignore or delay seeking emergency help.
  • Recognize common signs: Chest pressure or pain, sweating, nausea, shortness of breath, pain in arm, jaw, or back.
  • Stop activity: Help the person sit down and rest in a comfortable position.
  • Call 102: Do not attempt to drive yourself or the person if symptoms are severe.
  • Loosen clothing: Open collars, belts, or tight garments to help breathing.
  • Give aspirin (if suitable): If not allergic and fully conscious, they can chew a standard tablet of aspirin.
  • Keep them calm: Encourage slow breathing and reassure them while waiting for help.
  • Monitor breathing: If they stop breathing or lose consciousness, start CPR.

üå°Ô∏è Heat Stroke - Extreme Heat Exposure

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è High body temperature, confusion, or unconsciousness in hot weather is an emergency. Call 102. Quick cooling can save a life.
  • Move to a cool place: Take the person to shade or an air-conditioned area.
  • Lay them down: Help the person lie down with head slightly raised.
  • Remove extra clothing: Loosen or remove jackets, shoes, and unnecessary layers.
  • Cool the body: Apply cool, wet cloths on the neck, armpits, and groin.
  • Use a fan: Fan the person to increase cooling through evaporation.
  • Give fluids (if awake): Offer small sips of cool water or oral rehydration solution.
  • Do not give: Avoid very cold drinks, caffeine, or alcohol.
  • Watch for worsening: If they become confused, unconscious, or stop sweating, call 102 urgently.
